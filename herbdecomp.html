Herbivore - Decomposer feedbacks

I am working to understand how animals that eat plants (herbvivores) and animals that eat decomposing matter (detritivores) impact ecosystems.

Our experimental cages testing the combined impact of grasshoppers and earthworms on old-field plants and nutrient cycling.

Animals feeding on plants and animals that feed on dead plants* are linked through shared resources and a common nutrient cycle. In different ecosystems the identity of these types of animals changes, but thier role in nutrient movements is analagous.

Experiment on grasshoppers and earthworms

In New England old-fields the dominant herbivores are grasshoppers and dominant decomposers are earthworms and rolly-polies. These animals feed on the plants before and after their death, respectively, so can both influence how much the plants can grow. Grasshoppers eat living plants and change the types of plants and the amount they can grow aboveground. Earthworms increase the decomposition of dead plant leaves and recycle more nutrients for plants.** Rolly-polies also feed on dead leaves but their impact on plants and nutrient cycling can be very different from earthworms, because they also feed directly on the microorganisms that decompose leaves.

Our experimental design in cages with grasshoppers Melanoplus femurrubrum and earthworms in the genera Lumbricus and Apporectodea.

I ran a four year experiment to examine how grasshoppers and earthworms jointly impact plant communities and the nutrients in soil. My results demonstrate that the strong feedbacks we expect to arise from interactions between these animals do not actually occur. I built a new model of ecosystem nutrient cycling to figure out why feedbacks did not develop. My results show that big pools of soil nutrients and established differences in the types of plants in each area can buffer feedbacks for decades. My work also contributes to understanding how non-native earthworms impact our ecosystems (e.g. 1).

One of our experimental cages with plants starting to grow in 2016.

Experiment on grasshoppers and rolly-polies

I have also been studying how the link from grasshoppers to decomposers through plant leaf litter can change old-fields. A common hypothesis in our theory of herbivore - decomposer interactions is that herbivores make leaves difficult to eat and this slows down how quickly decomposers like rolly-polies can decompose them into the nutrients that help plants grow. Unfortunately, we don't know a lot yet about how animal decomposers respond to the changes in leaves caused by herbivores and we also don't know how much of an impact these changes in leaf decomposition actually have on plant growth.

I ran an experiment to explore both of these questions. 2 I found that rolly-polies care more about the nutreints (nitorgen) in the leaves than herbivore defenses and that these decomposing leaves don't have an immediate impact (in 1 year) on whether plants have enough nutrients to grow in our fields. Using a simulation model, I tested different hypotheses about the reason why plant nutrient uptake and growth did not change. I found that there was enough nitrogen available from the soil and plant root stores, so the relatively small increase coming from leaf litter was not enough to matter.

My big litter bags--cages where I decomposed different types of leaf litter to see how rolly-polies and decomposition changed the growth of plants.

* Dead plants and animals are called detritus. Animals that consume dead things are called detritivores.
** Many earthworms in northern North America are non-native species and some are having a negative effect on our forests!

Herbivore - Decomposer feedbacks

I am working in Connecticut old-fields to understand how animals such as grasshoppers, rolly-pollies, and earthworms jointly impact ecosystems.

Our experimental cages testing the combined impact of grasshoppers and earthworms on old-field plants and nutrient cycling.

Animals feeding on plants and animals that feed on dead plants* are linked through shared resources and a common nutrient cycle. In different ecosystems the identity of these types of animals changes, but thier role in nutrient movements is somewhat similar. In New England old-fields the dominant herbivores are grasshoppers and dominant decomposers are earthworms and rolly-polies.

These animals feed on the plants at different stages of their lives, but all influence how much the plants can grow. Grasshoppers eat living plants and change the types of plants and the amount they can grow aboveground. Earthworms, as many of us know, increase the decomposition of dead plant leaves and recycle more nutrients for plants.** Rolly-polies also feed on dead leaves but their impact on plants and nutrient cycling can be very different from earthworms.

Our experimental design in cages with grasshoppers Melanoplus femurrubrum and earthworms in the genera Lumbricus and Apporectodea.

Experiment on grasshoppers and earthworms

I have been running an experiment since 2016 to examine how grasshoppers and earthworms jointly impact plant communities and the nutrients in soil. My experiment will contribute to a much larger group of amazing work on how herbivores and decomposers interact and how non-native earthworms impact our ecosystems (e.g. 1). In old-fields I expect grasshoppers and earthworms to both increase the amount of nitrogen cycling and therefore promote the growth of grasses and other plants that excel in high-nutrient environments. (e.g. 2, 3). Thus far our data show that earthworms are changing the plant community in our cages away from legumes (e.g. clover) that loose out in high nitrogen environments.

One of our experimental cages with plants starting to grow in 2016.

Experiment on grasshoppers and rolly-polies

I have also been studying how the link from grasshoppers to decomposers through plant leaf litter can change old-fields. A common hypothesis in our theory of herbivore - decomposer interactions is that herbivores make leaves difficult to eat and this slows down how quickly decomposers like rolly-polies can decompose them into the nutrients that help plants grow. Unfortunately, we don't know a lot yet about how animals decomposers respond to the changes in leaves caused by herbivores and we also don't know how much of an impact these changes in leaf decomposition actually has on plant growth. My experiment explored both of these questions. I found that rolly-polies care more about the nutreints (nitorgen) in the leaves than their herbivore defenses and that these decomposing leaves don't have an immediate impact (in 1 year) on whether plants have enough nutrients to grow in our fields. ***

My big litter bags--cages where I decomposed different types of leaf litter to see how rolly-polies and decomposition changed the growth of plants.

* Dead plants and animals are called detritus. Animals that consume dead things are called detritivores.
**Earthworms in northern North America are non-native species and some are having a negative effect on our forests!
